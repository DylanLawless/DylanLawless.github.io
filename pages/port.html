Genomic analysis gallery

Complex genomic analysis for research and precision medicine requires numerous methods for robust statistical validation. However, to fully digest the raw data and present results that are both actionable and understandable, data-tailored presentation is critical. Presentations must by accurate, self-contained, and simplified. Often, the best data may be complex, multi-dimensional, or contain novel information that does not conform to standardised presentations. This gallery contains some examples of both simple and complex visualisations that we have previously published. This page is under live development - text descriptions and links to primary literature will be available shortly.

Protein network analysis for patients with shared phenotypes.
A splice site variant causes dominant autoinflammatory disease. (A) The pedigree includes Sanger sequences showing inheritance pattern, (B) cDNA sequence shows the outcome on transcript, (c) the effect on protein haploinsufficiency, (D) coding sequence illustration.
Variants in an autoinflammatory disease-causing protein are predicted based on population genetics data and illustrated against the multiple, critical functional domains in the protein. Residues that are highly conserved and prone to de novo mutation are most likely to require clinical intervention. Thus, pre-emptive treatment options are devised.
Combined reporting of raw data and the complete analysis for population genetics studies. This report contains all data and analysis scripts to reproduce the results and figures as illustrated in this plan.
Visualise novel analysis methods; predicted amino acid variation in critical immune proteins.
Visualise novel analysis methods; variant pathogenicity compared probability of variant in patients.
Variants that are predicted to be the most clinically-relevant are compared to the proteins structure (RAG protein complex binding DNA). The predicted residues were statistically validated as being functionally involved in DNA binding, however the visualisation presents this data for clearer understanding; likelihood of variants presenting as disease in humans; blue - not likely, yellow - mild, orange - moderate, red - most likely. DNA strands are shown in green as they are subjected to recombination during immune system development.
